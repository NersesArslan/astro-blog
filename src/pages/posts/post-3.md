I've been spending a great deal of money lately. I'm usually a pretty frugal person. But the past few weeks have been insane. I've had a blast hanging out with some friends over the weekends. But I got to start finding other ways hanging out without spending too much money by eating out or watching movies.

As a matter of fact, it may be a good idea for me to find other ways to get out of the house by myself to explore Los Angeles without spending a ton of money on eating out by myself, which I also have been doing a lot lately. Spending money on gas alone isn't an issue. It just adds when I keep buying stuff when I'm out. I got to be careful, even though I have quite a lot of savings and a relatively sizable inheritance to give me enough breathing room. My income from my job is low and it does enough to pay my rent and then some, but not all my bills. So I can't just blow all the money coming my way if I don't have any plans towards getting a better job or finding more jobs on top of my current job.

I did make some money selling 16 shares of NVDA about two weeks ago at $220 a share. I used the money to buy shares in KLAC, ASML and VRT. It made me wonder if I should continue working on that project I abandoned several months ago.

I was working this project that would help me identify value stocks for long-term investing. My thesis statement for this project was something along the lines of "what are the metrics for identifying value stocks for long-term investment?" And things got a bit tricky from there because what "value" meant was a bit vague and heavily dependent on the context of each company's industry, business model and market capitalization.

I was spending a lot of time asking chatGPT questions about how to build code for each feature. I ended up copy pasting and debugging a lot of python code more than actually reading the code myself. It felt like cheating and in a way it was. I'd have functions with names that let me know what they were for, but when something stopped working, I had to copy paste the entire code of a file because I didn't understand what any of the code was doing. It was honestly embarrassing, even though I wasn't working with anyone. I felt ashamed at my own laziness and at my outsourcing my thinking to an LLM, instead of making an effort to learn how to build a basic features of my project with python.

I did learn a lot with this project, even though I was using chatGPT to write the code. I kept asking it questions about how to best organize different parts of the project as it grew in complexity and I started getting more excited as it was able to return some robust financial metrics of the semiconductor and tech companies whose tickers I fed into its codebase.

There was a part of me that felt genuinely excited at the prospect of earning money building software that would identify value that would give me compounded returns over the long-term. Mymind went on to many different directions from there. Suppose I could actually figure out a thorough and rigorous system to identify value stocks for long term investment, what if I could then move on to devise a system to identify moonshot stocks that would return a maximally high return of a short period of time with the least probability of failure? A project like _that_ seemed a bit more fanciful and appealed to the part of me that liked to find a way to monetize my tendency to think of possibilities rather than execute actual ideas. But all the same these ideas stirred up an excitement in me that was all too dormant.
