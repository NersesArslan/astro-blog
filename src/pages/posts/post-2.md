I've been wanting to find different ways of earning money and carving out a career path for myself since I started working my first full time job in Los Angeles.

I really wanted to get into software development because it appeared to be one of the strongest career paths to get into at the time. I was 29, with only half a year in the US and only a few months into my jewelry e-commerce job in Downtown Los Angeles. I realized that I just didn't want to do that job for the foreseable future. It was depressing commuting to it job day in and day out and having just a little bit of free time for myself when I wasn't doing errands on the weekend to read a book or take walks in my neighborhood.

I remember that moment I was sitting in Gentei-an's library vividly, sitting there quietly with a good book. After a couple of weeks and months of that same routine, I looked up one day and asked myself "is this my life now?"

I recall waking up one day around May of 2022 with this vision of wanting to build a life for myself. I decided I wanted to take writing more seriously. After all, I was highly educated, I was and still am a voracious reader. I knew I was a good writer.

My biggest obstacles were losing interest and maintaing discipline and consistency once I started working on a project. I was able to find ways to finish papers and longer essays in college, however dissatisfied I was with most of their quality, because of deadlines and my having to account for my work to a professor. But I found it incredibly difficult to write about anything when I was writing for myself, with no one to offer me feedback or hold me accountable.

It seems as though writing as a skill was something that I've learned through institutions like my school and college. I've definitely experimented and played with different forms and even picked up a habit of writing short stories when I was in high school. I took up journaling several years ago at the suggestion of my college counselor and made a habit to stick to it to help me make sense of my life and mind.

I had made deliberate decisions on what to write about and what subject to specialize in during my college years as I got older. But when it make to serious writing, that is, writing of actual substance that I took a lot of time to not just write but to carefully re-read and revise—I had to, in some sense, needed it to be backed up by some institution for me to take it seriously.

Because if all that I'm doing is writing for myself, I get instantly bored by the editing and revising stage. I've written some really good drafts of what could be an auto-biographical creative non-fiction piece called _Los Angeles is a Whore_. I also wrote the beginning of an extended meditative piece that's based on my friendship with a close friend from college called _Hosam_ that could go on to be a great first short story. I know that I have the makings of a great writer. I am just not putting in the work because I am not surrounding myself with other readers and writers.

I find it difficult as it is to share some of these works with my closest friends. So I am even more hesitant to try to share my writing with other writers and readers in book clubs and writer's workshops. I also am struggling with this pseudo-impostor syndrome and self-sabotaging mindset that's somehow drilled into me since I moved to the U.S. that says something along the lines of "you had your chance to be a creative writer and intellectual when you were in college in Lebanon living with your mom. Now you're an adult that has to worry about rent and other responsibilities. Your priority is to figure out how to earn a living and stop this fantasy of wanting to become a writer." And that more than anything is what's making me hestitant to invest any more time and energy into my desire to write, let alone find ways to monetize it.

But I made a conscious effort the other day to write in my journal what it is I've been naturally drawn to in the past several weeks and months in my day to day life that gives my life meaning. Nabokov's _Ada or Ardor_. _The Romesh Ranganathan Show_. I particularly enjoyed his episode with Louis Theroux. That episode in particular made me want to watch Theroux's Netflix documentary _Inside the Manosphere_. I had already listened to some of Theroux's podcast episodes and recall that I thoroughly enjoyed his episode with the British comedian Jimmy Carr.

I loved listening to the Art of Darkness podcast episodes on _C.S. Lewis_ and _J.R.R. Tolkien_. This podcast already has episodes on some of my favorite writers.

I've been watching Mad Men almost religiously. I'm a little digusted with how much I love this show. It makes me want to stop watching television altogether, even though I'm also watching season 3 of Euphoria on the side as the new season's episodes get released each Sunday.

I've been going to my Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu classes regularly and more frequently since the first belt day I attended last February. I've noticed improvements in my game even though I still struggle with the basics. I lately have noticed some newer beginners getting drawn to me. I also feel closer to some of the more experienced people there. I felt particularly touched when one of the coaches for the Gi-class, coach O, gave a speech about the importance of culture and tradition after one of our classes. He said something along the lines of how everyone showing up to class is reflecting the gym's culture whether they know it or not. That showing up late regularly will reflect something about the culture of the gym; that each and everyone of us, not just the coaches and the more experienced grapplers, have our parts to play in maintainin the culture of the gym. He said we feel the absence of the coach Ian, one of the co-founders of the gym and that he's counting on everyone, even the blue belts, to step up and help the coaches in every class to the best of their ability. Then he pointed to me and said what he told me at the beginning of class when he noticed me trying to help another white belt how to tie his belt. He said it's tradition. I taught him and now he taught someone else and then that person will pass it on to another. This is what it's all about.

I felt genuinely touched by his speech and his mentioning me in it. Even though coach O is, so to speak, rough around the edges, someone I wouldn't particularly hangout with or associate with outside of the gym because we come from very different backgrounds, I felt the depth and sincerity in his speech and I liked it when he kept repeating how much "[He] loves this shit" throughout his speech.

I obviously am finding meaning in things. I obviously have a desire to do things with my life. I'm just at this phase where I'm still figuring it out as I go along.

I went to Americana again the other day and walked into Barnes and Nobles. I stood by a pile of books and picked up a copy of _Project Hail Mary_. I didn't even intend to buy that book. I had made up my mind to buy that copy of _Experience_ by Martin Amis that I noticed the last time I was there. But a man told me the book I picked up was really good and after a brief back and forth I told him that I'd buy it. I ended up buying another book I originally did not intend to buy. Merlin Sheldrake's _Entangled Life_.

I spent $64 on three books! I splashed some cash on myself and I can't help but think but writing that previous post made me more aware about my tendency to deny myself simple pleasures. I'm often very generous when it comes to spending money with friends. But when it comes to myself, I'm often very frugal and stingy. If I'm going to live a more fuller life, one where I am going to be happier, healthier and more self-confident, then I'm going to need to be able to practice self-love and self-respect. I will on occasion find ways to treat myself like I would a good friend, even when I feel like I don't deserve it or am particularly worried about my financial future.

I am now 87 spages away from finishing _Ada or Ardor_. I want to end with one small sketch form today that made me hope that I can still practice mindfulness in my day to day life.

I went to Manhattan Beach this morning before my brazilian jiu-jitsu class. I took a regular lap from the pier to the esplanade and back to my car. But before I went back home I went to the park that divides Valley Drive and North Ardmore Avenue. I sat down on a concrete bench and opened where I last left off. As I was reading I was transported to Van Veen's world and his letters to Ada after Lucette's suicide. I would occasionally look up from my book and into the peacefully lined trees in my line of sight and I felt a deep peace inside and all around me. It was something that I've experienced when I used to practice Zazen. But I knew it was fleeting and I did not even try to hold on to it. I went back to my book until a line from one of Van's letters to Ada drew my attention that I had to write it down:

"I cannot express, dear Van, how unhappy I am, the more so as we never learned in the arbors of Ardis that such unhappiness could exist."

She didn't know such unhappiness could exist in the arbors of Ardis. Do any of us, in the arbors of our own respective Ardis's know what kind of pain and suffering awaits us in adulthood?

I love this shit.
